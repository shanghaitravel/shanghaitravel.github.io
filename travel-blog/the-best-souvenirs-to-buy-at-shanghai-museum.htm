The Best Souvenirs to Buy at Shanghai Museum

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Every traveler knows the feeling. You’ve spent hours immersed in a world-class museum, your mind buzzing with the beauty of ancient bronzes, the serenity of classical paintings, and the intricate stories told by porcelain and jade. As you exit the galleries, you’re faced with the gift shop. This is not a moment of defeat, but of opportunity. The right souvenir is more than a trinket; it’s a tangible key to unlocking the memories of your experience. For the discerning visitor to the Shanghai Museum, one of China’s finest institutions, the gift shop is a final, curated gallery. Here, you don’t just buy a mass-produced magnet; you acquire a piece of cultural intelligence, a beautifully crafted narrative object. Let’s explore the best souvenirs to bring home, each a portal back to the heart of Chinese art and history.

The Philosophy of a Meaningful Souvenir

Before diving into the specific treasures, it’s worth understanding why the Shanghai Museum’s store stands apart. In an era of generic tourist traps, this museum has built its reputation on authenticity and scholarly dedication. Its replicas are often produced in collaboration with the same master craftsmen and workshops that restore the originals. When you purchase here, you are investing in a continuation of craft. You’re also practicing a more sustainable form of tourism—supporting cultural preservation directly. These items are conversation starters, designed to educate as much as to decorate. They answer the question: "What should I bring back from Shanghai?" with authority and taste.

Category 1: Replicas That Whisper History

This is where the museum truly shines. Instead of a cheap, plastic imitation, you can own a stunningly accurate reproduction of an artifact you admired in the galleries.

Bronze Wine Vessel (Zun or Jue) Replicas:

The museum’s ancient bronze collection is legendary. While you can’t take home a 3,000-year-old ritual vessel, you can find exquisite miniature or desk-sized replicas. Cast using traditional methods, often in lost-wax, these pieces capture the iconic taotie masks, intricate dragon patterns, and majestic green patina. A small zun on a bookshelf is a powerful statement of ancient artistry and makes for an utterly unique paperweight.

Tang Dynasty Tri-Color Glazed (Sancai) Ceramic Figures:

The lively camels, horses, and court ladies of the Tang Dynasty are icons of Chinese art. The museum’s replicas of these Sancai figures preserve the vibrant amber, green, and cream glazes. They bring a burst of historical joy and cosmopolitan Tang energy to any shelf. They are a fantastic alternative to the ubiquitous terracotta warrior, speaking to a period of trade, poetry, and cultural flourishing.

Category 2: The Scholar's Studio: Calligraphy and Painting

Chinese literati culture revolved around the "Four Treasures of the Study": the brush, ink, inkstone, and paper. The museum offers beautiful entry points into this world.

High-Quality Rubbings and Reproduction Scrolls:

Look for authorized reproductions of famous calligraphy works or paintings from the museum’s collection. These are printed on high-quality xuan paper or silk, capturing the nuance of each brushstroke. A framed reproduction of a misty mountain landscape or a powerful piece of calligraphy brings immediate serenity and sophistication to a wall. They are far more meaningful than a generic poster.

Decorative Inkstones and Brush Holders:

Even if you never grind an inkstick, a beautifully carved inkstone made of Duān or Shè stone is a sculptural object. Similarly, ceramic or bamboo brush holders decorated with classic motifs make elegant pen holders for a modern desk. They are functional art pieces that connect your daily work to a centuries-old tradition of contemplation and creation.

Wearable Wisdom: Jewelry and Adornments

For a more personal souvenir, the jewelry selection draws directly from museum artifacts.

Jade Pendants and Amulets:

Inspired by ancient bi discs, cong tubes, or dragon pendants, these pieces are crafted from nephrite jade or fine ceramics. They carry the symbolism of protection, virtue, and harmony that jade has held in Chinese culture for millennia. Wearing one is a subtle, stylish nod to deep history.

Cloisonné (Jingtai Lan) and Enamel Accessories:

Drawing from the museum’s superb Ming and Qing dynasty metalwork, you’ll find stunning cloisonné bracelets, earrings, and brooches. The brilliant blues, reds, and greens, separated by delicate metal wires, create dazzling patterns of florals, phoenixes, and geometric shapes. They are a wearable piece of imperial craftsmanship.

Modern Life, Ancient Patterns: Functional and Decorative Items

This is perhaps the most exciting category for the modern traveler, where ancient art meets contemporary design.

Porcelain with a Past:

Find teacups, bowls, and vases that faithfully reproduce the iconic blue-and-white patterns of the Yuan and Ming dynasties, or the delicate famille-rose enamels of the Qing. Enjoying your morning coffee from a cup inspired by a Chenghua period masterpiece is a daily luxury. These items bridge the gap between museum vitrine and dining table perfectly.

Silk Scarves and Ties:

Designers often take specific motifs from paintings, textiles, or ceramics and weave them into gorgeous silk accessories. A scarf featuring the curling vines from a porcelain pattern or the whimsical animals from a bronze vessel is both fashionable and deeply cultured. It’s a souvenir you can wear with pride on countless occasions.

Stationery and Desk Accessories:

From notebooks bound with brocade-like fabric to memo pads stamped with ancient seal script characters, the stationery selection is superb. Look for chopstick rests shaped like tiny antiquities, or magnets that are actually miniature replicas of famous coins from the museum’s numismatic collection. These small items make for excellent, portable gifts.

For the Curious Mind: Books and Media

Don’t overlook the museum’s publications. For English-speaking visitors, the catalogues raisonnés of their bronze, painting, and ceramic collections are definitive works. They are heavy, but they are the ultimate deep-dive souvenir. Smaller "highlights" books are more travel-friendly. Additionally, the museum offers multimedia collections of high-resolution images of their masterpieces, perfect for digital archives or creating your own custom prints at home.

Navigating Your Purchase: Tips for the Savvy Traveler

  • Budget Accordingly: Souvenirs here range from affordable postcards (¥10) to high-end replicas costing thousands. Set a budget, but remember you are paying for museum-authorized quality.
  • Seek the Story: Look for tags and descriptions that explain which specific artifact in the collection inspired the item. The more specific, the better.
  • Think About Transport: For fragile items like porcelain, the packaging is excellent, but consider bringing a small collapsible duffel in your suitcase for the journey home.
  • Gift with a Narrative: When buying for others, choose an item you can explain. A gift becomes more special when it comes with a 30-second story about the Song dynasty painting or the symbolism of the dragon it depicts.

The Shanghai Museum gift shop is a destination in itself, a final chapter in your cultural journey. It challenges the notion of the souvenir as a forgettable cliché. Instead, it offers objects of beauty, intellect, and connection. Whether it’s a jade pendant against your skin, a sip of tea from a Ming-style cup, or the gaze of a Tang ceramic horse on your mantle, these souvenirs do more than remind you of a visit. They keep the dialogue with history, art, and craftsmanship alive in your everyday world, long after your flight has left Pudong.
