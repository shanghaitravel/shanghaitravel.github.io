Shanghai Public Transport Guide: How to Get Around Easily

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Shanghai, a dazzling metropolis where futuristic skyscrapers meet historic shikumen alleyways, is a city best explored using its efficient public transport system. Whether you're visiting the Bund at sunrise, shopping in Nanjing Road, or sipping coffee in a French Concession café, knowing how to navigate Shanghai’s transit options will save you time—and maybe even a few headaches.

The Metro: Shanghai’s Backbone

Why the Metro is Your Best Friend

With 20 lines covering over 800 kilometers, the Shanghai Metro is the world’s largest rapid transit system by route length. It’s fast, affordable (fares start at 3 RMB), and signs are bilingual (English/Chinese). Key lines for tourists:

  • Line 1: Connects Shanghai Railway Station to Xujiahui (shopping) and People’s Square (museums).
  • Line 2: Runs through Lujiazui (Oriental Pearl Tower) and Hongqiao Airport.
  • Line 10: Hits the Old City (Yuyuan Garden) and the hip Xintiandi district.

Pro tip: Avoid rush hour (7:30–9:30 AM and 5–7 PM) unless you enjoy close-quarters socializing.

How to Pay

  • Single-journey tickets: Buy at vending machines (cash/WeChat Pay/Alipay).
  • Transport Card (Jiaotong Card): Reloadable, works on metro/buses/ferries. Refundable deposit.
  • Mobile Apps: Use "Metro Metropolis" or "Shanghai Metro" for real-time updates.

Buses: For the Adventurous Traveler

When to Take the Bus

While less English-friendly than the metro, buses reach areas like Tianzifang’s artsy lanes or the quieter Zhujiajiao water town. Key routes:

  • Bus 911: Vintage double-decker along Huaihai Road (prime shopping).
  • Sightseeing Bus: Hop-on/hop-off routes cover the Bund, Pudong, and more.

Bus Etiquette 101

  • Pay by cash (exact change) or scan QR codes via WeChat/Alipay.
  • Announcements are in Chinese—use Google Maps (offline mode) to track stops.

Taxis & Ride-Hailing: The Comfort Option

Didi vs. Traditional Cabs

  • Taxis: Flag down (light on roof = available). Meters start at 14 RMB. Say "dǎbiǎo" (use the meter) to avoid scams.
  • Didi Chuxing: China’s Uber. Link your WeChat/Alipay for cashless rides. Pro tip: Screenshot your destination in Chinese to show the driver.

Cross-River Hacks

Tunnels and bridges link Pudong/Puxi, but rush-hour traffic is brutal. Opt for the metro (Lines 2/4) or the Bund Sightseeing Tunnel (a trippy light-show experience, 50 RMB).

Two Wheels: Bikes & E-Scooters

Bike-Sharing Frenzy

  • Hellobike/Meituan: Scan QR codes to unlock bikes (1.5 RMB/30 mins). Perfect for short rides like exploring the French Concession.
  • Safety note: Bike lanes exist but beware of scooters weaving at lightning speed.

E-Scooters: For the Bold

Rent via apps like Niu (need a local SIM). Not recommended for beginners—Shanghai traffic is intense.

Ferries & Trains: Scenic Alternatives

Huangpu River Ferries

For 2 RMB, cross the river like a local. Best routes:
- Dongchang Road to Puxi: Sunset views of the Pearl Tower.
- Jinling Road Ferry: Near the Bund’s iconic skyline.

Day Trips by Train

  • Hongqiao Station: High-speed trains to Hangzhou (West Lake) in 1 hour.
  • Shanghai South Station: Slow trains to water towns like Zhouzhuang.

Accessibility & Pro Tips

Metro Accessibility

Most stations have elevators, but older lines (e.g., Line 1) may require stair climbing. Download "Accessible Shanghai" for barrier-free routes.

Survival Mandarin for Transit

  • "Qǐng wèn, __ zěnme zǒu?" (How do I get to __?)
  • "Xià yī zhàn shì nǎlǐ?" (What’s the next stop?)

Avoid These Mistakes

  • Ignoring peak hours: Metro queues can rival Disneyland lines.
  • Overlooking e-bikes: They’re silent but deadly (to pedestrians).
  • Forgetting cash: Some buses/taxis don’t take digital pay.

Shanghai’s transport web might seem daunting, but it’s a thrill to master. From neon-lit metro tunnels to breezy ferry rides, every journey adds to your adventure. So tap that transport card, hop on, and let the city’s rhythm guide you.
