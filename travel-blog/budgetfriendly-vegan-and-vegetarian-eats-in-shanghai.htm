Budget-Friendly Vegan and Vegetarian Eats in Shanghai

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Forget the image of Shanghai as just a glittering forest of skyscrapers and luxury boutiques. Beneath its dazzling modern surface pulses a city with a deep, delicious soul, and you don't need a hefty wallet to experience it. For the traveling vegan or vegetarian, Shanghai is a thrilling playground of flavors, where ancient Buddhist traditions meet a booming, conscious, modern food scene. Navigating this culinary landscape can be an adventure in itself, leading you down winding longtangs (alleyways) and into bustling local markets, far from the tourist traps. This guide is your key to eating incredibly well, supporting local spots, and diving deep into the authentic, budget-friendly heart of Shanghai's meat-free cuisine.

The Lay of the Land: Street Eats, Canteens, and Local Gems

The cornerstone of affordable eating in Shanghai is its vibrant street food culture and no-frills local eateries. While many classic street snacks are meat-heavy, a little knowledge unlocks a treasure trove.

Jianbing: The Ultimate Breakfast of Champions

No Shanghai experience is complete without a jianbing. This savory crepe is a spectacle: a batter of mung bean and wheat flour is spread thin on a giant griddle, an egg is cracked over it, then it's brushed with savory sauces, sprinkled with scallions, cilantro, and a crispy fried wonton skin (baocui), and folded into a portable packet. The default often includes a smear of fermented bean paste. Simply learn the phrase "bu yao tianmianjiang, bu yao la" (no sweet bean sauce, no meat) to get a vegan version. For 6-12 RMB, it’s the most satisfying, protein-packed breakfast you’ll find.

Vegetarian *Jiaozi* and *Baozi*

Steamed dumpling shops are on every corner. Look for signs that say 素食 (sùshí) meaning vegetarian. Common fillings include fragrant chives and egg, wood ear mushroom and cabbage, or leek and vermicelli. A steamer basket of these fluffy delights costs around 10-15 RMB. Shengjianbao, the pan-fried soup dumplings, are trickier as the soup is often meat-based, but dedicated vegetarian shengjian spots are popping up.

The Humble *Lāmiàn* Shop

The hand-pulled noodle shop is a sanctuary. While broths are typically beef or pork, you can almost always order 素菜面 (sùcài miàn)—vegetable noodles. This usually means a simple, clear, but deeply flavorful broth with wheat noodles, bok choy, and maybe a few mushrooms or tomatoes. It’s pure, comforting, and rarely costs more than 20 RMB.

Buddhist Temple Cuisine: A Spiritual and Culinary Must-Do

This is arguably the most unique and culturally rich vegan experience in Shanghai. Buddhist temple restaurants serve exquisite, entirely plant-based (sùshí) meals that mimic meat in form and texture using gluten (seitan), tofu, and mushrooms, a centuries-old tradition. It’s an absolute must for any visitor.

Longhua Temple Restaurant

Near the historic Longhua Pagoda, their cafeteria-style restaurant is a masterclass in affordable gourmet vegan food. Load up a tray with dishes like "sweet and sour pork" (made of wheat gluten), braised "goose," stir-fried mushrooms, and sublime soups. A heaping plate costs 25-40 RMB. The serene atmosphere, surrounded by monks and locals, is unforgettable.

Jing'an Temple Vegetarian Restaurant

Tucked within the golden hustle of Jing'an Temple, this spot offers a similar experience. After watching the incense swirl in the courtyard, descend to the basement for a feast. Their set meals are a fantastic deal, offering a curated taste of their delicate, artful cuisine for around 50 RMB.

Navigating Local Restaurants & Hotpot on a Budget

Don't be afraid to step into busy local restaurants filled with Shanghainese families. The key is knowing what to look for and how to order.

The "Vegetarian Section" & Must-Know Dishes

Most menus have a 素食 (sùshí) section. Staple, wallet-friendly orders include: * Kōngxīn cài (Water Spinach): Stir-fried with garlic. * Tiěbǎn dòufu (Iron Plate Tofu): Sizzling, spicy tofu and vegetables. * Qiézi dòujiǎo (Eggplant with Beans): A classic, often slightly sweet and savory stir-fry. * Mápó dòufu (Ask for sù de): The iconic Sichuan dish can be made vegan—request no meat and ensure they use a vegetable-based sauce. A plate typically ranges from 20-35 RMB. Two dishes plus rice make a perfect, cheap meal for two.

Vegetarian Hotpot: A Social Feast

Hotpot isn't just for meat-lovers. Budget chains like Haidilao are famous for service, but for a local price, seek out smaller spots. Create your own vegan paradise by loading up on: leafy greens, mushrooms (enoki, shiitake, wood ear), tofu, tofu skin, lotus root, sweet potato noodles, and corn. Choose a yuányāng guō (split pot) and get a mushroom or tomato broth on one side. Dipping sauce is where you shine: mix sesame paste, chopped garlic, cilantro, peanuts, and soy sauce for a flavor explosion. Per person cost can be as low as 50-80 RMB.

The Modern Vegan Wave: Affordable & Instagram-Worthy

Shanghai's young, health-conscious crowd has fueled a rise in cool, casual, and still reasonably priced vegan cafes.

Wugu Market & Similar Health Food Stores

Places like Wugu Market are lifesavers. They offer a small cafe menu with vegan burgers, bowls, and wraps (40-60 RMB), plus a grocery section stocked with local and imported vegan snacks, plant milks, and sauces—perfect for self-catering.

Bowl & Smoothie Cafes

In areas like the Former French Concession and Jing'an, you'll find countless cafes serving massive, colorful Buddha bowls, smoothie bowls, and avocado toast. While pricier than street food (50-80 RMB per bowl), they are a fantastic, healthy recharge during a day of sightseeing and offer reliable Wi-Fi and Western comforts.

Pro-Tips for the Budget-Conscious Traveler

  • Embrace the Apps: Use Meituan or Ele.me for food delivery and discovery. The photo-based menus are easy to navigate, and prices are often lower than in-store. Look for the "素食" tag.
  • Market Marvels: Visit local wet markets like Jiashan Market. It's a sensory overload of fresh, dirt-cheap produce, tofu of every texture, and dried mushrooms. Grab some fruit for snacks.
  • Bakery Bonanza: Chinese bakeries like 85°C or Kaisa have hidden gems: sweet red bean buns, coconut buns, or plain toast. Always ask "yǒu nǎi yǒu dàn ma?" (does it have milk or egg?).
  • Learn Key Phrases: Beyond "sùshí," knowing "wǒ chī sù" (I eat vegetarian) and "bù yào ròu, bù yào yú, bù yào jīdàn, bù yào niúnǎi" (no meat, no fish, no egg, no milk) is empowering.
  • Water is Your Friend: Skip sugary drinks and bottled water. Carry a reusable bottle and boil tap water or use a filter. Tea is always free and safe at restaurants.

The true joy of eating vegan in Shanghai isn't just in the savings—it's in the journey. It leads you to share a temple table with a praying local, to master the art of pointing at a steaming basket of dumplings, and to discover that the most memorable flavors often come from the simplest, most honest places. So grab your chopsticks, venture forth with curiosity, and let Shanghai's vibrant, plant-based heart nourish both your body and your travel spirit.
