Shanghai COVID Travel Guide: From Testing to Accommodation

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

The pulsating heart of modern China, Shanghai, is once again open and radiating its unique energy. The city of towering skyscrapers, historic shikumen lanes, and world-class cuisine is welcoming travelers back. However, the landscape of travel has evolved. A successful trip to Shanghai now hinges on smart, informed planning that blends the classic highlights with new, post-pandemic protocols. This isn't about restrictions dampening your fun; it's about savvy preparation unlocking a seamless and profoundly rewarding Shanghai experience. Consider this your essential playbook for navigating the city's current rhythm, from pre-departure tests to finding your perfect home base.

Pre-Flight Prep: Your Digital Key to Shanghai

Gone are the days of just booking a flight and packing a bag. Your journey begins online, well before you reach the airport.

1. Visa and the Vital Health Code

First, ensure your visa is in order. With tourism visas being processed again, check the latest requirements from the Chinese embassy or consulate in your country. The cornerstone of your entry, however, is the China Health Declaration Code. This is filled out via the customs website or app (or on paper upon arrival, but digital is faster). You'll need to provide your flight details, accommodation in China, and a health self-declaration. Upon completion, you'll receive a QR code—screenshot it! This code is checked repeatedly at your departure airport and upon landing in Shanghai. It's your first digital key to the city.

2. The Testing Question: To PCR or Not to PCR?

This is the most fluid part of travel planning. As of now, pre-departure COVID-19 PCR tests are NOT required for entry into China. This is a huge change and simplifies logistics dramatically. However, this policy is subject to change. The non-negotiable rule is this: Check the official requirements 48-72 hours before your flight. Airlines and Chinese border authorities will have the final, up-to-date word. While a test may not be needed, carrying a few self-test antigen kits in your luggage is a wise traveler's move for personal peace of mind during your stay.

Touching Down at PVG or SHA: A Streamlined Arrival

The arrival process at Shanghai Pudong (PVG) or Hongqiao (SHA) airports is notably smoother than in previous years. Expect the standard immigration and customs procedures. The Health Declaration QR code you filled out earlier will be scanned. There is currently no mandatory on-arrival testing or quarantine. Temperature checks might be in place. The key is to have all your documents—passport, visa, and health code—readily accessible. Once you clear customs, you're free to dive into the city.

Navigating the City: Masks, Apps, and Mobility

Shanghai operates with a pragmatic approach. The city moves fast, and so should you.

Metro, Didi, and Getting Around

Shanghai's metro system is the lifeline of the city. To use it, you'll need the "Metro Big Shanghai" app or a physical transportation card. While mask mandates are not strictly enforced everywhere, carrying a high-quality mask is essential. You will see a significant portion of the local population wearing masks in crowded, enclosed spaces like metro carriages, buses, and department stores. It is a sign of social responsibility and common courtesy to follow suit in these settings. For taxis, Didi (China's Uber) is indispensable. Download the Didi app and link an international payment method before you arrive. It's a lifesaver.

Attractions, Dining, and the Daily Scan

The era of ubiquitous venue code scanning has largely passed. You generally will not need to scan a QR code to enter most public attractions, malls, or restaurants. However, exceptions exist. Some major tourist sites, museums, or large event spaces might have their own simple health check or registration procedures. Always have your phone charged and ready. When dining, menus are almost always accessed via QR code on the table, so a WeChat app with a working scan function is incredibly useful.

Where to Stay: Accommodation for the New Travel Era

Shanghai's accommodation scene is more vibrant than ever, catering to every travel style. Your choice of neighborhood will define your experience.

The Bund & People's Square: Classic Grandeur

For first-timers who want the iconic postcard view, staying near The Bund is unbeatable. You'll find historic luxury hotels like the Fairmont Peace Hotel and the Waldorf Astoria, offering timeless elegance and those breathtaking vistas of Pudong's skyline. This area is perfect for feeling the pulse of old and new Shanghai, with easy access to the Nanjing Road pedestrian street and major museums. It's a hub of tourist energy.

Former French Concession: Chic and Walkable

If you prefer tree-lined avenues, boutique shopping, and charming cafes, the Former French Concession is your district. Accommodation here ranges from designer hotels to intimate serviced apartments and upscale hostels. It's a neighborhood for wandering, discovering hidden longtang alleys, and enjoying a more relaxed, atmospheric side of the city. Areas like Xintiandi and Tianzifang are within easy reach.

Pudong: The Futuristic Heartbeat

For a truly modern experience, stay in Pudong's Lujiazui financial district. Hotels like the Park Hyatt, the Mandarin Oriental, or the Ritz-Carlton place you in the clouds, literally. You'll be steps from the Shanghai Tower, the Oriental Pearl TV Tower, and the Super Brand Mall. This area feels hyper-efficient, sleek, and is fantastic for families or business travelers. The view of The Bund across the river at night is spectacular.

Jing'an & Xuhui: Cosmopolitan Hubs

Jing'an, with its namesake temple nestled amidst skyscrapers, is a dynamic blend of spiritual calm and urban buzz. It's a major shopping and dining hub. Xuhui, particularly around the Shanghai Library and Jiaotong University areas, is an upscale residential and commercial zone with great parks, cafes, and a sophisticated vibe. Both offer excellent metro connectivity and a "live like a local" feel with international comforts.

Embracing the Shanghai Spirit: Hotspots & Hidden Gems

Beyond the logistics, Shanghai is about immersion. Here’s what’s trending and timeless.

The Rise of "City Walks" and Micro-Discovery

The hottest trend among locals and in-the-know travelers is the "City Walk." Ditch the rigid itinerary and pick a thematic route. Wander along the Suzhou Creek, exploring revitalized warehouses turned into art galleries and cafes. Trace the history of the Jewish refugees in the Hongkou district. Explore the "Columbia Circle" in the former French Concession, a restored 1930s country club complex now housing restaurants and design studios. This is how you find the soul of modern Shanghai.

Culinary Adventures: From Street Food to Haute Cuisine

Shanghai's food scene is explosive. Yes, you must try xiaolongbao (soup dumplings). For a classic experience, join the queue at a institution like Jia Jia Tang Bao. But go further. Explore the breakfast culture on Shaanxi South Road. Book a table at a sophisticated Huaiyang cuisine restaurant. Dive into the vibrant international food scene in neighborhoods like Hongkou (for Turkish) or Gubei (for Japanese and Korean). The city is a global pantry.

Cultural Pulse: Art, Fashion, and Nightlife

The West Bund has cemented itself as Shanghai's premier cultural corridor. The Long Museum, the West Bund Museum (in partnership with the Centre Pompidou), and countless galleries are must-visits for art lovers. For fashion, the newly developed Zhang Yuan historic district offers a stunning mix of restored shikumen architecture housing high-end boutiques and concept stores. As for nightlife, the speakeasy bar scene is world-class. Seek out hidden doors behind phone booths or barber shops for inventive cocktails and unforgettable evenings.

The current chapter of Shanghai travel is one of reconnection and rediscovery. It asks for a little more forethought but rewards you with a city that is accessible, vibrant, and eager to share its stories. By mastering the practical steps—from staying updated on entry rules to choosing the right neighborhood—you free yourself to fully absorb the dazzling contrast, the warm hospitality, and the relentless innovation that is Shanghai. Pack your bags, your masks, and your sense of adventure. The Pearl of the Orient is waiting.
