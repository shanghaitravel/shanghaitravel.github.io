Don’t Forget These! Shanghai Travel Checklist for First-Timers

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Shanghai is a dazzling blend of ancient traditions and futuristic skylines, making it a must-visit for travelers. But before you dive into the neon-lit streets of The Bund or lose yourself in the historic alleys of Tianzifang, here’s a comprehensive checklist to ensure your trip is smooth, enjoyable, and unforgettable.

Essential Travel Documents

Passport and Visa

Double-check that your passport is valid for at least six months beyond your travel dates. Most visitors to China require a visa, so apply well in advance. The process can take weeks, especially during peak seasons.

Copies of Important Documents

Scan your passport, visa, travel insurance, and hotel reservations. Store digital copies in your email or cloud storage and carry physical backups.

Travel Insurance

Medical emergencies or trip cancellations can happen. Opt for a policy that covers healthcare, theft, and flight delays.

Money Matters

Local Currency (RMB)

While credit cards are accepted in upscale places, cash is king in local markets and small eateries. Exchange some RMB before arriving or withdraw from ATMs (avoid airport ATMs for better rates).

Mobile Payment Apps

WeChat Pay and Alipay dominate Shanghai’s cashless economy. Link your international card or ask a local friend to help top up your balance.

Connectivity

VPN (Virtual Private Network)

Google, Instagram, and WhatsApp are blocked in China. Download a reliable VPN like ExpressVPN or NordVPN before landing to stay connected.

Local SIM Card

Purchase a prepaid SIM at Pudong Airport or China Mobile stores. Data plans are affordable and essential for navigation (Google Maps won’t work—use Baidu Maps or Amap instead).

Packing Smart

Weather-Appropriate Clothing

Shanghai’s weather swings dramatically:
- Spring (Mar-May): Light layers, rain jacket.
- Summer (Jun-Aug): Breathable fabrics, sunscreen, umbrella.
- Autumn (Sep-Nov): Sweaters, comfortable shoes.
- Winter (Dec-Feb): Heavy coat, scarf, gloves.

Comfortable Footwear

Expect lots of walking—opt for cushioned sneakers or supportive sandals.

Power Adapter

China uses Type A/C plugs (220V). Bring a universal adapter if your devices don’t match.

Must-Have Apps

Didi (Ridesharing)

Shanghai’s metro is efficient, but Didi (China’s Uber) is handy for late-night rides.

Pleco (Translation)

Overcome language barriers with this offline English-Chinese dictionary.

Dianping (Food Guide)

Find top-rated restaurants and street food stalls—think of it as Yelp for China.

Cultural Know-How

Basic Mandarin Phrases

While many young Shanghainese speak English, learning a few phrases helps:
- Nǐ hǎo (Hello)
- Xièxiè (Thank you)
- Duōshǎo qián? (How much?)

Tipping Etiquette

Tipping isn’t customary in China. Leaving money may confuse staff, except in high-end hotels.

Health and Safety

Mask and Air Quality Apps

Shanghai’s air quality fluctuates. Pack masks and check apps like IQAir for real-time updates.

Medications

Bring prescriptions and over-the-counter meds (decongestants, pain relievers). Pharmacies may not stock familiar brands.

Tap Water Warning

Avoid drinking tap water. Stick to bottled or boiled water, even when brushing teeth.

Unmissable Experiences

The Bund at Night

Marvel at Shanghai’s iconic skyline illuminated against the Huangpu River. Arrive early to snag a prime photo spot.

Yu Garden and Bazaar

Wander through Ming Dynasty-era gardens, then haggle for souvenirs in the bustling bazaar.

French Concession

Tree-lined streets, boutique cafes, and colonial architecture make this area perfect for a leisurely afternoon.

Foodie Adventures

Xiaolongbao (Soup Dumplings)

Head to Jia Jia Tang Bao or Din Tai Fung for these juicy, broth-filled delights.

Shengjianbao (Pan-Fried Buns)

Crispy bottoms, tender tops—try them at Yang’s Fry Dumplings.

Street Food at Wujiang Road

Sample jianbing (savory crepes) and chuanr (spicy skewers) from local vendors.

Shopping Hotspots

Nanjing Road

A shopper’s paradise with everything from luxury brands to quirky souvenirs.

Tianzifang

Artsy lanes filled with indie boutiques, handmade crafts, and hipster cafes.

AP Plaza (Fake Market)

Bargain hard for "designer" goods, watches, and electronics—it’s all part of the fun.

Transportation Tips

Metro System

Shanghai’s metro is cheap, clean, and extensive. Grab a rechargeable交通卡 (Jiāotōng Card) for hassle-free rides.

Maglev Train

Experience the world’s fastest commercial train (431 km/h) from Pudong Airport to Longyang Road.

Bike Rentals

Mobike and Hello Bike offer cheap, eco-friendly ways to explore—just unlock via their apps.

Etiquette and Customs

Queueing

Lines can be chaotic. Stand your ground politely but firmly.

Business Cards

If exchanging cards, present/receive them with both hands as a sign of respect.

Temple Visits

Dress modestly and avoid loud noises in sacred spaces like Jing’an Temple.

Hidden Gems

Propaganda Poster Art Centre

A quirky museum showcasing Mao-era posters—off the radar but fascinating.

Zhujiajiao Water Town

Escape the city for a day to this "Venice of Shanghai," complete with gondolas and ancient bridges.

Long Museum (West Bund)

Contemporary art lovers shouldn’t miss this sleek gallery by the Huangpu River.

Final Reminders

  • Check Public Holidays: Avoid National Week (Oct 1-7)—crowds are overwhelming.
  • Stay Charged: Carry a portable charger; outlets are scarce in public areas.
  • Be Street-Smart: Watch for pickpockets in crowded spots like People’s Square.

Shanghai awaits with open arms—armed with this checklist, you’re ready to conquer the city like a pro!
