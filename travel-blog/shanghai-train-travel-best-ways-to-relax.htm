Shanghai Train Travel: Best Ways to Relax

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

The rhythm of Shanghai is a palpable force. It’s in the constant hum of the Maglev, the symphony of horns on the Yan'an Elevated Road, and the relentless, beautiful chaos of the Bund. For the traveler, this energy is intoxicating, but it can also be utterly draining. This is where the magic of the train journey comes in. More than just a means of transit, the train network radiating from Shanghai’s colossal stations—Hongqiao and Shanghai—is a gateway to serenity, a moving sanctuary where the journey itself becomes the destination for relaxation. Forget the frantic dash to the airport; this is about reclaiming travel as a peaceful, immersive experience.

The very act of boarding a train initiates a shift in pace. As the city’s iconic skyline—a forest of glass and steel spearheaded by the Shanghai Tower—begins to recede, replaced by the emerald-green tapestry of the Yangtze River Delta, your heart rate slows. You are physically and mentally disconnecting, a feeling rarely afforded by air travel. This is the first and most profound step in relaxing: the conscious departure.

Crafting Your Moving Sanctuary: The Art of the Journey

The key to a relaxing train trip from Shanghai lies in intentionality. It’s about choosing the right service and preparing not just your luggage, but your mindset.

Choosing Your Class: From Serene Comfort to Lavish Indulgence

China's high-speed rail (Gaotie) network offers a tiered experience catering to every relaxation need.

  • Second Class: While economical, it’s far from Spartan. The seats are comfortable, there’s ample legroom, and the carriages are clean and climate-controlled. For shorter journeys to places like Suzhou or Hangzhou, it’s perfectly adequate for gazing out the window and letting your mind wander.
  • First Class: This is where the relaxation quotient increases significantly. With a 2-2 seat configuration instead of 2-3, you gain precious personal space. The seats are wider, recline further, and the atmosphere is noticeably quieter. It’s the ideal balance of comfort and value for journeys over two hours.
  • Business Class: For the ultimate in train-bound tranquility, Business Class is a revelation. Spacious, pod-like seats that swivel, recline into a near-horizontal position, and come with plush footrests transform your seat into a private lounge. Attentive service, complimentary meals and snacks, and a near-silent environment make the journey feel less like travel and more like a luxury retreat on rails.

The Pre-Journey Ritual: Shanghai's Stations as Gateways

Hongqiao Railway Station is a destination in itself, a marvel of modern logistics. To avoid last-minute stress, arrive at least 45 minutes early. But don’t just sit and wait. Turn this into part of the experience. Grab a genuinely excellent flat white from a local café like Manner Coffee, browse the well-stocked bookshops for a novel for the journey, or pick up a box of exquisite snacks from a vendor like Kee Wah Bakery. This transforms pre-departure time from anxious waiting to pleasant anticipation.

Iconic Relaxing Routes from Shanghai

The true beauty of train travel from Shanghai is the diversity of destinations accessible within a few hours, each offering a unique flavor of relaxation.

The Water Town Circuit: Suzhou and Tongli

A mere 25-minute high-speed train ride transports you from Shanghai’s hyper-modernity to the timeless canals of Suzhou, the "Venice of the East."

  • The Journey: It’s over almost as soon as it begins, a brief, smooth interlude of green fields before you arrive.
  • The Relaxation: Suzhou is famed for its classical gardens, like the Humble Administrator’s Garden and the Lingering Garden. These are not merely parks; they are masterpieces of landscape design, intended for contemplation and harmony. Spend a day meandering through these UNESCO World Heritage sites, where every rock, pond, and pavilion is placed to inspire peace. Follow this with a gentle boat ride along the ancient canals, the gentle lapping of water against stone being the only sound to break the silence. For the ultimate relaxation, check into a boutique hotel in a restored traditional building and let the old-world charm wash over you.

An even more immersive water town experience is a 30-minute train ride to Tongli. Tongli feels more lived-in and authentic. Spend a night here after the day-trippers have left. Wandering the dimly lit, cobbled lanes in the evening, with the reflections of red lanterns shimmering in the black water, is a profoundly calming experience.

The Lakeside Retreat: Hangzhou

In under an hour, you can be in Hangzhou, a city that embodies poetic beauty. The journey itself is a preview, skirting the edges of the lush countryside.

  • The Journey: Relax into your seat with a cup of tea, watching the landscape become progressively greener.
  • The Relaxation: The centerpiece of Hangzhou is West Lake (Xi Hu). The best way to experience its serenity is to rent a bicycle and cycle the tree-lined causeways that crisscross the lake, or take a leisurely boat to one of its islands. Visit the Lingyin Temple, a active Buddhist monastery nestled in a forested hillside. The scent of incense and the sight of devoted pilgrims create an atmosphere of deep tranquility. No trip to Hangzhou is complete without participating in a tea ceremony at a plantation in the nearby Longjing hills, learning about and tasting the world-famous Dragon Well tea. This is a sensory relaxation that engages sight, smell, and taste.

The Bamboo Forest Immersion: Anji

For those seeking a deeper connection with nature, a two-hour train ride south leads to Anji, the "Bamboo Capital of China."

  • The Journey: This is a longer, more meditative trip. Use this time to read, listen to a full album, or simply watch the world go by.
  • The Relaxation: Visit the Canglong Bai Bamboo Forest, a breathtaking sea of green where scenes from Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon were filmed. Walking or taking a golf cart through the towering bamboo groves, with sunlight filtering through the canopy, is a form of natural therapy. The air is fresh and clean, and the only sounds are the rustling of a million bamboo leaves in the wind—a natural white noise machine. For an added layer of wellness, stay in one of the many eco-lodges or boutique hotels that offer yoga sessions with views of the bamboo-covered mountains.

Onboard Relaxation: Making the Most of Your Time on the Rails

The destination is only half the story. The time spent on the train is your personal, uninterrupted sanctuary.

  • Digital Detox and Mindful Observation: While the trains have reliable WiFi, challenge yourself to a partial digital detox. Instead of scrolling, look out the window. The ever-changing landscape of China is a captivating show—from the industrial outskirts giving way to farmland, to the dramatic karst mountains of the south. It’s a live documentary on the country's vastness and variety.
  • The Perfect Travel Playlist: Curate a soundtrack for your journey. The gentle, rhythmic sound of the train on the tracks is the perfect base layer for ambient music, calming classical pieces, or acoustic folk.
  • Train Picnics: Instead of relying solely on the train cart, pack a small picnic. A thermos of high-quality tea, some fresh fruit, and a few pastries from a Shanghai bakery can elevate your journey into a delightful mobile feast.
  • Mindful Reading and Journaling: A train journey is the perfect opportunity to finally dive into that novel you’ve been meaning to read or to jot down your thoughts and observations in a travel journal. The lack of distractions makes for focused, fulfilling engagement.

Beyond the Obvious: The Slow Train Alternative

While the Gaotie is the star, don’t overlook the older, slower "D" trains or even the classic overnight sleeper services for longer trips to places like Beijing or Xi’an. These journeys force a different kind of relaxation—one dictated by a slower clock. On an overnight train, you fall asleep to the gentle rocking motion in one city and wake up to an entirely new world outside your window hundreds of miles away. It’s a romantic, deeply satisfying way to travel that connects you to a older tradition of rail travel, where the distance covered was felt not just in miles, but in time.

The constant, gentle percussion of wheels on track becomes a mantra, lulling you into a state of calm. The world outside your window transforms from a blur into a detailed painting of rural life, distant mountains, and sprawling cities. You arrive not just at a new destination, but in a new state of mind—refreshed, centered, and reminded that sometimes, the best way to truly experience a place is to slowly, peacefully, travel through it.
