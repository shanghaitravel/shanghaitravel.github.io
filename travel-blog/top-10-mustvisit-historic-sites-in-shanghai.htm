Top 10 Must-Visit Historic Sites in Shanghai

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Shanghai is a city perpetually sprinting towards the future, a neon-drenched metropolis of soaring skyscrapers. Yet, to understand its breathtaking velocity, you must first visit the places where it began. Its history isn't just preserved behind glass; it's woven into the vibrant fabric of its streets, its cuisine, and its relentless energy. This isn't a journey through dusty relics, but a discovery of living narratives. From colonial concessions to revolutionary hideouts and serene classical gardens, here are the top 10 historic sites that reveal the soul of Shanghai.

The Bund: Shanghai's Iconic Waterfront Chronicle

No visit to Shanghai is complete without a stroll along the Bund. This famous waterfront is the ultimate visual summary of the city's past and present.

A Gallery of Architectural Ambition

Facing the Huangpu River, you'll see a spectacular row of 52 buildings spanning Gothic, Baroque, Romanesque, and Art Deco styles. This is the legacy of the International Settlement and French Concession era. Key landmarks include the clock-towered Customs House, the neoclassical HSBC Building (with its stunning mosaic dome ceiling), and the sleek Peace Hotel, a Jazz Age icon. This stretch earned the nickname "the museum of international buildings."

Across the River: The Future's Skyline

Turn around, and the view delivers Shanghai's dramatic punchline: the futuristic skyline of Pudong, with the Oriental Pearl Tower, Shanghai Tower, and Jin Mao Tower. This juxtaposition is the essence of Shanghai. For the best experience, visit at dusk when the colonial edifices are bathed in golden light, and stay as they and the Pudong towers ignite into a synchronized electric symphony—a truly unforgettable spectacle.

Yu Garden & The Old City: Ming Dynasty Majesty

Step through a moon gate and leave the 21st century behind. The Yu Garden, a restored 16th-century classical Chinese garden, is an oasis of serene design.

Rockeries, Pavilions, and Peace

Built during the Ming Dynasty by a government officer for his parents, the garden's name translates to "Garden of Happiness." It's a masterpiece of balance, featuring intricate rockeries (like the famed Jade Rock), koi-filled ponds, dragon-lined walls, and carefully framed views from every pavilion and corridor. It represents the philosophical and aesthetic ideals of a scholar's retreat.

The Bustling Bazaar of Old Shanghai

The garden is nestled within the Old City, once surrounded by walls. The surrounding bazaar is a whirlwind of activity. Snag a xiaolongbao (soup dumpling) at the legendary Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant, browse traditional handicrafts, and soak in the atmosphere. At the heart of it all stands the vibrant City God Temple, a Taoist temple complex that has been the spiritual center of the old city for centuries.

Former French Concession: Romance and Revolution

Wander the tree-lined avenues of the Former French Concession, and you feel a shift in pace. This area is history repurposed as lifestyle.

Shikumen and Chic Boutiques

Here, history is in the architecture. Look for Shikumen ("stone gate") houses, unique hybrids blending Western terraced house forms with Chinese courtyard layouts. The best-preserved example is Xintiandi, a dining and entertainment district where Shikumen have been meticulously restored to house trendy cafes, galleries, and boutiques. It's a prime example of Shanghai's talent for adaptive reuse.

Historic Residences and Cultural Footprints

This district was also a haven for intellectuals and revolutionaries. You can visit the Former Residence of Sun Yat-sen, where the "Father of Modern China" lived and worked. Nearby, the Site of the First National Congress of the Communist Party of China is a modest Shikumen house where the party was founded in 1921—a stark contrast to the nation it would later shape. Don't miss Fuxing Park, a classic French-style park where locals practice tai chi, dance, and socialize.

Jing'an Temple: Ancient Serenity Amidst Skyscrapers

The ultimate symbol of Shanghai's layered identity: a 1,800-year-old golden Buddhist temple sits peacefully in a plaza surrounded by gleaming luxury malls and office towers. Originally founded in 247 AD, the current structures are reconstructions, but the spiritual significance is palpable. The main hall, with its magnificent Jade Buddha and giant golden Buddha statue, is a place of active worship. The constant hum of incense and chanting against a backdrop of urban roar is a uniquely Shanghai experience.

Longhua Temple & Pagoda: Shanghai's Oldest Monastery

For a deeper, less commercialized spiritual history, head to Longhua. Shanghai's oldest and largest monastery complex dates back to 242 AD, according to legend. The beautiful Seven-Story Pagoda is the city's oldest surviving structure. The temple's architecture follows the classic Song Dynasty symmetrical layout. Its history is somber, having witnessed numerous conflicts, but today it's a serene place, especially beautiful during the Longhua Temple Fair and when the peach blossoms in its garden bloom in spring.

Shanghai Jewish Refugees Museum: A Sanctuary in Stormy Times

One of Shanghai's most poignant stories is told in the Tilanqiao neighborhood. During World War II, Shanghai was an open port, offering a rare haven to approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees fleeing the Holocaust. The museum is housed in the former Ohel Moshe Synagogue. Its exhibits detail the difficult but resilient lives of the community who lived, worked, and worshipped in this "Shanghai Ghetto." It's a powerful testament to human survival and Shanghai's unexpected role as a sanctuary.

Zhujiajiao Water Town: A Day Trip to "Shanghai's Venice"

Escape the urban core and travel back in time with a short trip to one of the region's ancient water towns. Zhujiajiao, with a history spanning 1,700 years, is a charming labyrinth of canals, stone bridges (like the iconic Fangsheng Bridge), and Ming/Qing dynasty architecture. Glide along the canals on a wooden boat, explore the Kezhi Garden, and sample local street food. It offers a tranquil glimpse into the Jiangnan water town culture that shaped the region long before Shanghai became a global metropolis.

Shanghai Propaganda Poster Art Centre: A Unique Collection

Tucked away in the basement of an unassuming apartment building, this private museum is a hidden gem. It houses an extraordinary collection of original propaganda posters from the Mao era (1949-1979). The vibrant, stylized artwork—promoting revolution, industrial achievement, and socialist ideals—offers a fascinating, visually striking window into China's modern political history and the power of visual communication during a transformative period.

St. Ignatius Cathedral: Gothic Grandeur in Xujiahui

In the bustling Xujiahui district stands a stunning red-brick Gothic cathedral. Built by French Jesuits between 1906 and 1910, it was once the largest cathedral in East Asia. Its twin spires, stained-glass windows, and imposing facade speak to the influence of the French Concession. Severely damaged during the Cultural Revolution and meticulously restored, it now serves as an active church and a peaceful respite. The contrast of its silhouette against modern shopping malls is breathtaking.

Tianzifang & M50: Industrial Heritage Reborn

Shanghai's history isn't only imperial or colonial; it's also industrial. These two districts showcase the city's creative recycling of its 20th-century factory past.

Tianzifang's Artsy Lanes

Born from renovated Shikumen factories and labyrinthine alleyways in the French Concession, Tianzifang is a thriving arts and crafts enclave. It's more organic and gritty than Xintiandi. You'll find independent designer shops, artist studios, quirky cafes, and lively bars tucked into every corner, offering a tactile sense of local creative energy.

M50 Art District on Moganshan Road

For a purer art scene, head to M50. This complex of abandoned textile mills on the Suzhou Creek has been transformed into Shanghai's premier contemporary art district. Galleries, studios, and graffiti-covered walls occupy the rough industrial spaces. It's a dynamic spot to see cutting-edge Chinese art and witness how the city's physical history constantly fuels its cultural evolution.
