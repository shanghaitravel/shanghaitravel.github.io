Shanghai’s Best Taro Dishes: A Root Vegetable Tour

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

The true soul of Shanghai’s food scene isn't always found sizzling in a wok or nestled in a steaming soup dumpling. Sometimes, it’s hidden in the humble, unassuming embrace of a root vegetable. We’re talking about taro, the starchy, slightly sweet, and incredibly versatile tuber that forms the backbone of countless iconic Shanghai dishes. To truly understand this city's culinary heart, you must embark on a tour guided by this knobby, earthy treasure. This is not just a food crawl; it's a journey through texture, tradition, and the very essence of Shanghainese comfort.

For the uninitiated, taro might seem like a bland cousin of the potato. But in the hands of Shanghai’s chefs, it transforms. It can be silky smooth, crispy and airy, or dense and cake-like. It soaks up rich sauces like a dream and provides a subtle, nutty counterpoint to bold, savory flavors. From bustling breakfast stalls to the polished tables of historic restaurants, taro is a constant, comforting presence. So, let's pull back the curtain on this culinary star and map out the ultimate taro tour through the labyrinthine streets of Shanghai.

The Savory Soul: Taro in Main Courses

This is where taro truly shines, moving from a supporting actor to a co-star, often upstaging the meat it’s cooked with. These dishes are the cornerstone of a family meal and a testament to the Shanghainese love for slow-braised, deeply flavorful food.

1. The Iconic: Taro and Pork (Yútou shāo ròu)

No taro tour is complete without paying homage to this legendary dish. Imagine: generous chunks of pork belly, slow-braised until the fat is gelatinous and the meat is fall-apart tender. Now, add in wedges of taro that have been fried briefly before stewing. The magic happens in the pot. The taro absorbs the rich, sweet, and savory soy-based sauce, becoming impossibly creamy and flavorful, while simultaneously thickening the sauce to a luxurious consistency. The taro doesn’t just accompany the pork; it becomes its equal, offering a fluffy, savory-sweet bite that cuts through the richness of the meat. You’ll find this masterpiece in nearly every Běnběng cài (local Shanghai cuisine) restaurant. For an authentic experience, head to a classic spot like Old Jesse or 1221 Restaurant, where the dish has been perfected over generations.

2. The Crispy Delight: Taro Duck

A dish that showcases the incredible textural versatility of taro. Here, taro is mashed and seasoned, then used to encase a layer of seasoned duck meat. The whole creation is then deep-fried to a spectacular golden brown. The result is a symphony of contrasts. The exterior is shatteringly crisp, giving way to a soft, hot, and fluffy taro layer, which then reveals the savory, succulent duck within. It’s a dish of surprise and elegance, often served during festive occasions but available year-round at sophisticated Shanghainese establishments. It’s a must-try for anyone wanting to see taro’s more refined side.

3. The Humble Stew: Taro with Soy Sauce (Yóutú jiàng)

Sometimes, the simplest preparations are the most comforting. In many home-style kitchens and smaller lilong eateries, you’ll find taro simply stewed in soy sauce, perhaps with a bit of scallion and oil. Without the distraction of meat, the true, nutty flavor of the taro gets to be the star. The edges become soft and melt-in-your-mouth, while the core retains a slight bite. It’s a vegetarian-friendly powerhouse of flavor and a beautiful example of how Shanghai cuisine can elevate the most basic ingredient into something profoundly satisfying.

The Sweet Escape: Taro in Desserts and Snacks

Shanghai’s love affair with taro doesn’t end with the main course. The root vegetable effortlessly crosses over into the world of sweets, creating some of the city’s most beloved snacks and desserts that are as Instagrammable as they are delicious.

1. The Modern Phenomenon: Taro Paste Pastries and Drinks

Walk through any modern Shanghai shopping mall or trendy food street, and you’ll be greeted by the unmistakable lavender hue of taro paste. This sweet, smooth concoction, made from steamed and mashed taro, sugar, and sometimes purple sweet potato for color, has become a national obsession. It’s the filling of choice for flaky pastries, soft breads, and fluffy cakes.

But the real star of the show is the Taro Ball Milk Tea, a bubble tea innovation that has taken the city by storm. Instead of traditional tapioca pearls, these drinks feature chewy, QQ taro balls made from taro starch, often paired with a rich taro paste and fresh milk. Sipping on this creamy, textured drink while wandering through the French Concession or along the Bund is a quintessential modern Shanghai experience. Chains like Heytea and Lelecha have entire menus dedicated to taro, offering a dizzying array of options for the tuber enthusiast.

2. The Traditional Treasure: Taro Cakes (Yùbǐng)

For a more traditional sweet treat, seek out Shanghainese taro cakes. These are not the pan-fried savory cakes found in Cantonese dim sum, but rather steamed or pan-fried cakes where shredded taro is mixed with glutinous rice flour and sugar, then often topped with nuts or candied fruits. The texture is delightfully sticky, chewy, and dense, with the subtle sweetness of the taro shining through. They are a common sight in older bakeries and specialist dessert shops, a nostalgic bite of old Shanghai.

3. The Festive Specialty: Taro in Mooncakes

During the Mid-Autumn Festival, the mooncake landscape in Shanghai expands beyond the traditional lotus and red bean pastes. Taro paste mooncakes, particularly the flaky "Su" style ones, are a highly sought-after variety. The delicate, buttery pastry layers crumble at the touch, revealing a smooth, not-too-sweet taro filling that is both elegant and comforting. Picking up a box of these from a renowned bakery like Godly or Xinghualou is a seasonal ritual for many Shanghainese families.

Where to Embark on Your Taro Tour: A Neighborhood Guide

Knowing the dishes is one thing; knowing where to find them is another. Here’s a quick guide to navigating Shanghai’s neighborhoods in pursuit of the perfect taro dish.

The Old City and Yu Garden: A Traditional Starting Point

The labyrinth of streets around Yu Garden is a fantastic place to begin. Here, amidst the traditional architecture and tourist buzz, you’ll find classic restaurants serving impeccable Taro and Pork. The atmosphere is chaotic and vibrant, offering a true taste of old Shanghai. Pop into a busy-looking restaurant where the locals are eating, and you’re almost guaranteed a memorable taro experience.

The French Concession: Trendy Treats and Hidden Gems

This is the epicenter of Shanghai’s modern taro trend. The tree-lined streets are dotted with chic bubble tea shops where you can grab that photogenic Taro Ball Milk Tea. But look closer, and you’ll also find small, unassuming Běnběng cài restaurants tucked away on side streets, serving phenomenal and affordable versions of Taro Duck and Taro with Soy Sauce. It’s the perfect neighborhood to experience the duality of taro in Shanghai—both its trendy and its traditional faces.

Jing'an and Former French Concession: Elevated Experiences

For a more upscale taro journey, the areas around Jing'an Temple and the western part of the Former French Concession are home to higher-end Shanghainese restaurants. Places like Fu 1088 or Fu 1015 offer refined, artistically plated versions of the classics. Their Taro and Pork might be more delicate, their Taro Duck more intricately prepared. It’s taro, dressed up for a special night out.

The story of taro in Shanghai is a story of texture, transformation, and deep-seated culinary wisdom. It’s a root that connects the steaming street-side woks to the polished tables of haute cuisine, the hurried breakfast of a local to the leisurely dessert of a tourist. So, on your next visit to Shanghai, look beyond the obvious. Ask for the menu items featuring this wonderful root. Let the humble taro guide you to the true, comforting, and unexpectedly delicious heart of the city. Your taste buds will thank you for the adventure.
