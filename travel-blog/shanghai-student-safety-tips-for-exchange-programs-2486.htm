Shanghai Student Safety: Tips for Exchange Programs

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Shanghai is one of the most dynamic cities in the world, blending ancient traditions with cutting-edge modernity. For students participating in exchange programs, it offers an unforgettable experience—bustling markets, towering skyscrapers, and a rich cultural heritage. However, navigating this metropolis safely requires awareness and preparation. Whether you're exploring The Bund, indulging in xiaolongbao, or attending classes at Fudan University, these safety tips will help you make the most of your time in Shanghai.

Understanding Shanghai’s Unique Environment

The Fast-Paced Urban Life

Shanghai moves at lightning speed. From the crowded subway lines to the vibrant nightlife in Xintiandi, the city never sleeps. While this energy is exhilarating, it can also be overwhelming for newcomers.

  • Public Transportation: The metro system is efficient but crowded during rush hours (7:30–9:30 AM and 5–7 PM). Keep your belongings secure to avoid pickpockets.
  • Traffic Awareness: Pedestrian signals aren’t always followed strictly. Look both ways before crossing, even at green lights.

Weather Considerations

Shanghai experiences four distinct seasons, each with its own challenges:

  • Summer (June–August): Hot and humid, with occasional typhoons. Stay hydrated and carry an umbrella.
  • Winter (December–February): Chilly and damp. Layer up, as indoor heating isn’t always consistent.

Staying Safe in Public Spaces

Navigating Popular Tourist Spots

Shanghai’s attractions draw millions of visitors yearly. While these spots are generally safe, staying vigilant is key.

The Bund and Nanjing Road

  • Crowds: These areas are packed, especially on weekends. Keep your phone and wallet in a zipped bag.
  • Scams: Beware of overly friendly strangers offering "discounted" tours or souvenirs.

Tianzifang and Yu Garden

  • Narrow Alleys: These charming areas can get cramped. Watch your step and be mindful of your surroundings.
  • Vendors: Bargaining is common, but avoid flashing large amounts of cash.

Nightlife Safety

Shanghai’s nightlife is legendary, but it’s important to party responsibly.

  • Clubs and Bars: Stick to well-known venues in areas like Xintiandi or the French Concession.
  • Transport at Night: Use Didi (China’s Uber) or official taxis. Avoid unlicensed cabs.

Health and Emergency Preparedness

Food and Water Safety

Shanghai’s street food is delicious, but take precautions:

  • Street Vendors: Opt for stalls with long lines—it usually means the food is fresh.
  • Tap Water: Avoid drinking it. Stick to bottled or boiled water.

Medical Resources

  • Hospitals: International hospitals like Shanghai United Family Hospital offer English-speaking staff.
  • Pharmacies: Watson’s and local pharmacies carry common medicines, but bring any prescription medications from home.

Cultural Etiquette and Communication

Respecting Local Customs

  • Gift-Giving: If invited to a local’s home, bring a small gift like fruit or tea.
  • Dining Etiquette: Don’t stick chopsticks upright in rice—it’s considered bad luck.

Language Tips

While many young Shanghainese speak English, learning basic Mandarin phrases helps:

  • Hello: Nǐ hǎo (你好)
  • Thank you: Xièxiè (谢谢)
  • Help!: Jiùmìng! (救命!)

Digital Safety and Connectivity

Internet Access

  • VPNs: Apps like WeChat and Alipay work without a VPN, but access to Google, Instagram, etc., requires one.
  • Public Wi-Fi: Avoid logging into sensitive accounts on unsecured networks.

Essential Apps

  • Didi: For safe rides.
  • MetroMan Shanghai: Navigate the subway with ease.
  • Pleco: A must-have Mandarin dictionary.

Financial Safety

Handling Money

  • Cash vs. Mobile Payments: Alipay and WeChat Pay dominate, but carry some cash for small vendors.
  • ATMs: Use machines inside banks to avoid skimming devices.

Avoiding Scams

  • Fake Tickets: Purchase attraction tickets from official websites or your program coordinators.
  • Overcharging: Always check receipts, especially in tourist-heavy areas.

Building a Support Network

Connecting with Locals and Peers

  • University Clubs: Join student groups to meet both locals and fellow exchange students.
  • Language Exchange: Platforms like HelloTalk can help you practice Mandarin while making friends.

Emergency Contacts

  • Police: 110
  • Ambulance/Fire: 119
  • Your Country’s Consulate: Save the contact info in your phone.

Shanghai is a city of endless possibilities, and with these safety tips, you’ll be well-equipped to enjoy every moment of your exchange program. From savoring shengjianbao to marveling at the skyline from the Shanghai Tower, your adventure awaits—just remember to stay smart and stay safe!
